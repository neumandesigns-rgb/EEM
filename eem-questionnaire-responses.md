EEM exists to put the Bible into the hands of people who want it but cannot get it. For more than 60 years we’ve published and distributed Bibles in dozens of 33+ languages in 35+ nations across Eastern Europe, the Middle East, and beyond. We work through trusted local partners so that when someone asks for God’s Word, the answer can simply be “yes.”

Brooke: EEM works with a vast network of churches and organizations to provide people free access to Scripture in their heart language access Europe and Asia. It started with a few hundred books a year in the 1960s and has grown to fulfill more than two million requests. People are hungry for God’s Word, and we want to partner with others in the Gospel to meet that demand in the language of their heart.

Bob: EEM is a Christian ministry that embodies the Biblical spirit of the parable of the sower.  EEM sows the Word of God through the distribution of millions of Bibles and biblical literature each year in over 33 languages and 35+ nations.  Once people get and read the Bible in their native language, there is a transformation in their heart as they understand God’s love for them now and in eternity.

5. Gwen and Gayle Hensley smuggled Bibles behind the Iron Curtain at a time when simply owning one was illegal. What was the belief underneath that decision? And is that same belief still the engine of what EEM does today, or has something shifted?  
   Answer here…

They believed that every person deserves access to God’s Word, no matter the risk or cost. That same conviction still drives EEM today. The methods have changed, but the belief has not: people need the Bible, and we will do whatever we can to make sure they receive it.

Brooke: At the deepest level, Gwen’s decision was not driven merely by a passion for distribution, or even by courage alone. It was driven by the conviction that these were people deeply loved by God, and that without access to the gospel and the truth of Scripture, they would remain cut off from the very thing their souls most needed. When Gwen stood in that Romanian village and saw an entire community sharing a single Bible, his heart broke because God’s heart had already broken for them. He believed that the lost mattered, that eternity mattered, and that God’s Word was the vehicle through which people could come to know the love and forgiveness of God. That is why he could say, “If it’s the Lord’s will, and I believe it is, I’m going to see that these people get a Bible in their language.” The engine beneath the impossible dream was love: love born from having first been loved by God and forgiven of sin.

And yes, I think that same belief is still the engine of what EEM does today. The scale has changed. The methods have changed. The political landscape has changed. But the animating conviction has not changed. EEM still exists because God breaks our hearts for what breaks His heart: the lost, the overlooked, the unreached, the people still longing to know they are loved by Him. We are still moved by the same truth that moved Gwen: that people need access to God’s Word so they can know the God who loves them, be forgiven, and be brought into relationship with Him. If anything has shifted, it is not the belief itself but the scope of how God has expanded it. What began as a burden for people behind the Iron Curtain has grown into a wider vision to bring God’s Word to millions across nations, languages, generations, and circumstances. But it is still the same fire. We caught a glimpse of God’s heart through Gwen’s eyes, and by the Holy Spirit that same fire continues to drive the mission today.

Bob:  I concur with Dirk’s answer.

14. EEM operates from the conviction that God is the one doing the real work and EEM is just a vehicle. How does that shape the way you communicate? Does it create any tension with things like urgency, fundraising, or measuring impact?  
    Answer here…

It keeps humility at the center of everything we say and do. We communicate the mission with urgency and clarity, but we are always careful to give God the credit for the real transformation that happens when people encounter His Word.

But we try to frame those things simply as stewardship. Our responsibility is to faithfully provide access to the Bible and be transparent about the opportunity; God is the one who changes hearts and lives.

Brooke: Yes, God is working and leading the way. Yes, I think it shapes the way we communicate in a profound way. If EEM truly believes God is the one doing the real work and we are just a vehicle, then our communication should be marked by humility, dependence, and stewardship. We are not the hero. We are trying to faithfully tell the story of what God is doing through His Word, through open doors, and through the people who are receiving and sowing Scripture.

At the same time, that conviction does create real tension. There is urgency because people are dying every day without knowing the gospel, and there are people actively seeking God whose requests for Bibles can go unfulfilled simply because we do not yet have the funds to meet every opportunity. So while we trust God deeply, that does not remove the responsibility to communicate clearly, fundraise faithfully, and ask how we can better steward marketing, branding, creative, and strategy so that fewer open doors are missed.

On the other hand, we also believe He guides through practical strategy, through stronger branding, better storytelling, more effective copy, and wiser communication. In some ways, our dependence on God may have made certain parts of that slower to develop, but we are trying to grow in both faith and practical stewardship.

And when it comes to measuring impact, there is tension there too, because not everything God is doing can be easily quantified. Some fruit is visible: the addict set free, the father returning home, the sick child healed, the person awakening to a new calling, the believer choosing quiet obedience, the church or family changed by engagement with Scripture. Some of it is dramatic, and some of it is hidden. So we measure what we can, because stewardship matters, but we also know the deepest impact of God’s Word in a person’s life is not always something that fits neatly into a report.

3. EEM works in a specific geography, Eastern Europe and the neighboring regions. That specificity is a strategic choice. Why this part of the world? And how central is that geography to EEM's identity going forward, especially as the work expands beyond it?  
   Answer here…

Brooke: Eastern Europe was central to EEM’s beginning because there was a real and urgent need there. Under communism, the Bible was restricted or illegal in many places, and God led the original team to respond by printing in Vienna and smuggling Scripture into communist nations, especially Ukraine and Russia. After the wall fell in 1989, requests began pouring in, and what started as a focused response to one region grew through relationships, need, and open doors across Eastern Europe.
