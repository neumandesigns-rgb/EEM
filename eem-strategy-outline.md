**PART 1 — WHO EEM IS**

**01\. Identity Statement** Not a mission statement. A clear, present-tense articulation of what EEM *is* as an organization — its nature, its posture in the world, and its theological conviction. This is the room everything else is built from.

**02\. Vision Statement** The preferred future EEM is moving toward. What the world looks like if EEM's work succeeds fully. Bold, clear, directional.

**03\. Mission Statement** What EEM does, for whom, and why. One or two sentences. Built to be usable internally and externally.

**04\. Core Values** The convictions that govern how EEM operates — not aspirational platitudes, but actual behaviors that can be observed and held accountable. Defined with real-world application, not just labels.

**05\. Founding Story & Legacy** The Iron Curtain origin, Gwen and Gayle Hensley, what EEM risked and why. This is a strategic asset being underutilized. Framed as a living legacy, not a historical footnote.

---

**PART 2 — STRATEGIC POSITIONING**

**06\. The Landscape: Why This Work, Why Now** The full human and spiritual picture of Eastern Europe and beyond — war, political instability, spiritual hunger, Bible inaccessibility. The urgency case, grounded in reality, not just statistics.

**07\. Brand Positioning Statement** How EEM is distinctly perceived relative to comparable organizations. What EEM owns that no one else can claim. The intersection of legacy, scale, specificity, and access. Written as a strategic tool, not marketing copy.

**08\. The Core Differentiator** The single most important thing that makes EEM unlike anything else in its space — the *why this and not that* answer. This drives every downstream decision.

**09\. Brand Promise** The specific, ownable commitment EEM makes to its audiences — what people can count on from every EEM interaction, every piece of communication, every campaign.

**10\. Brand Archetype** The archetypal character EEM embodies. This gives the brand a consistent emotional and relational identity. Explored with nuance — EEM likely sits in tension between two archetypes, and that tension is part of what makes it distinct.

**11\. The Brand Idea** The single organizing thought underneath everything EEM says and does. Not a tagline — the *strategic idea* a tagline would express. This is where we determine if "The Bible. We want everyone to get it." is that idea, or if it's the expression of something deeper.

---

**PART 3 — COMPETITIVE LANDSCAPE**

**12\. The Organizational Landscape** An honest look at comparable Bible/mission organizations — American Bible Society, Gideons, Wycliffe, Biblica, Open Doors, Bible League International. Not to position EEM against them as competitors, but to clarify what territory EEM distinctly occupies and what it must own more clearly to avoid category confusion. Framed as "fellow laborers, distinct calling."

**13\. EEM's Unique Territory** What EEM owns that no one else can — regional specificity, 60+ year legacy in this geography, the proprietary partner network, the publishing \+ delivery integration, the founding story. What would be lost from the global church if EEM stopped existing.

**14\. The Perception Gap: Internal vs. External** How EEM sees itself versus how it is currently perceived by donors, partners, and the broader public. Where those gap and why. This section names the problem your proposal diagnosed with strategic precision.

---

**PART 4 — AUDIENCE STRATEGY**

**15\. Audience Framework** The full audience ecosystem mapped and segmented — not just demographics, but psychographics, faith posture, relationship with missions, and what they need to feel before they act.

**16\. Primary Audience: The Established Faithful Donor** Older, long-tenured supporters. What they believe, what they value, what sustains their loyalty, what risks losing it. How to speak to them with honor while moving them toward greater engagement and legacy giving.

**17\. Secondary Audience: The Mission-Minded Next Generation** Younger adults (25-40) who are globally aware, skeptical of institutions, moved by stories, and looking for a place to put their idealism. What activates them, what turns them off, and what EEM must do to earn their trust and mobilize their participation.

**18\. Supporting Audiences** Church leaders and pastors as multiplication partners. Philanthropists and major donors. Christian educators. Legacy/estate giving prospects. Each mapped with their specific entry point and messaging need.

**19\. Audience Journey Map** The full relational arc from first awareness to long-term partnership — for each primary audience. What EEM needs to say and do at each stage: Awareness → Curiosity → First Engagement → Trust → Active Support → Deep Partnership/Legacy. Where EEM currently succeeds and where it loses people.

---

**PART 5 — MESSAGING STRATEGY**

**20\. Core Message Architecture** The hierarchy of what EEM communicates — what always leads, what supports, what proves, and what invites. Structured so any communicator can build on-brand messages without a designer or strategist in the room.

**21\. Voice & Tone** How EEM sounds. Defined with behavioral specificity: what EEM says, how it says it, and what it never sounds like. Includes register shifts across contexts — campaign urgency vs. story-driven content vs. donor stewardship vs. prayer communication.

**22\. Messaging by Audience** Tailored messaging emphasis for each key audience segment. What leads for long-time donors. What leads for younger audiences. What activates a pastor versus a philanthropist. What reassures versus what inspires versus what mobilizes.

**23\. Campaign Messaging Framework** How Hope Campaign and Bibles for Kids connect back to the parent brand — and how future campaigns can be built within this system. What every campaign must do, say, and feel to remain recognizably EEM.

**24\. The Encounter / Embrace / Magnify Framework: Refined** Whether to retain, reframe, or rebuild this narrative scaffold. If retained, it gets sharpened into a genuinely usable tool — for story selection, content sequencing, donor journey mapping, and campaign structure.

---

**PART 6 — BRAND CHARACTER**

**25\. Brand Personality** The human character of EEM — described as if EEM were a person. How it shows up in a room, what it says, how it treats people, what it cares about. Paired explicitly with what EEM is *not*, so the guardrails are clear.

**26\. Brand Tensions & Guardrails** The honest, productive tensions EEM must hold: institutional trust *and* relational warmth. Global scale *and* individual story. 60-year legacy *and* present-day urgency. Established audience *and* next-generation activation. Each tension is named, and the guardrails for navigating it are defined.

**27\. What EEM Stands For (and Against)** Not political — theological and missional. What EEM will not compromise, will not drift toward, will not do in the name of relevance. This creates internal clarity and external credibility.

---

**PART 7 — VISUAL STRATEGY**

**28\. Visual Positioning Statement** Where EEM sits on the visual landscape relative to comparable organizations — and the intentional direction its visual identity should move. This is the strategic rationale *before* any design decisions.

**29\. Visual Personality & Principles** The character of EEM's visual expression — warmth, movement, intimacy, credibility. Translated into observable design principles that any designer or content creator can apply.

**30\. Photography Philosophy** The most important visual asset EEM has is its field photography. This section defines what EEM's photography should feel like, what it must communicate, and how to direct it. Human-first, on-the-ground, relational. Aligned to Encounter / Embrace / Magnify if that framework is retained.

**31\. Typography & Color Direction** Strategic rationale for type and color — not a style guide, but the *why* behind visual choices that the Phase 2 design system will execute.

**32\. Expression Hierarchy: Core vs. Campaign** What stays constant across all EEM expression. What flexes for campaigns. How Bibles for Kids and Hope Campaign feel distinct *and* unmistakably EEM. Rules for what gets the most visual energy, what stays grounded.

---

**PART 8 — EXPERIENCE STRATEGY**

**33\. The Donor Experience Journey** End-to-end experience mapping for EEM's primary supporter — from first hearing about EEM to becoming a Hope Line Community monthly giver to making a legacy gift. What they feel at each stage. Where the current experience breaks down. What excellence looks like.

**34\. Touchpoint Audit** Every major touchpoint EEM owns — website, email, social, podcast, print, events, giving confirmation, stewardship communications — mapped against the brand strategy. Where they're aligned, where they're creating confusion, and what needs to shift.

**35\. Content & Storytelling Strategy** How EEM's story ecosystem — impact stories, 55:11 podcast, social, video, magazine — functions as a strategic whole. What each channel is for, how they hand off to each other, and how stories move people along the journey.

---

**PART 9 — FUTURE GROWTH**

**36\. Strategic Growth Priorities** Where EEM has the most strategic opportunity over the next 3-5 years — audience growth, geographic expansion, digital engagement, monthly giving growth, legacy cultivation. Framed as brand strategy, not fundraising strategy.

**37\. Next Generation Activation Strategy** A dedicated, specific plan for how EEM earns the trust and mobilizes the participation of younger donors and advocates — not just a demographic target, but a genuine strategic shift in engagement, storytelling, and community building.

**38\. Brand Evolution Guardrails** How EEM grows and adapts without losing what makes it EEM. What can change, what cannot. How campaigns can push the brand forward without fragmenting it. How the brand stays alive over time.

---

**PART 10 — APPLICATION**

**39\. Decision Filters** A short, plain-language set of evaluative questions any staff member or partner can use to assess whether a piece of work — design, content, campaign, communication — aligns with EEM's strategy. Built for daily use without a strategist present.

**40\. This Is What It Looks Like** Concrete, specific examples of the strategy applied to real decisions. This is where the framework becomes tangible — not hypothetical application, but actual guidance.
